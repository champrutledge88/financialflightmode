# FFM Financial Flight Score Methodology and SOP v1

## Purpose

The Financial Flight Score is an educational diagnostic created by Financial Flight Mode to help everyday people quickly understand their monthly money situation.

The score is not meant to be complicated for the pilot. The internal methodology can be detailed, but the public experience must stay simple.

Pilot experience:

1. Enter monthly numbers.
2. Receive one score.
3. See one status.
4. Understand the reason quickly.
5. Take three next moves.

## Simplicity Standard

The methodology lives behind the cockpit.

Pilots should not see long financial breakdowns, complex formulas, or overwhelming research explanations. They should see clear signals and practical next steps.

Every public explanation should be understandable within 10 seconds.

## What The Score Measures

The Financial Flight Score measures financial control and readiness across five practical signals:

1. Cash Flow Control
2. Savings System
3. Emergency Runway
4. Debt Load
5. Wealth Fuel

## What The Score Does Not Measure

The score is not:

- A credit score
- A lending decision
- A financial plan
- Investment advice
- Tax advice
- Legal advice
- A judgment of personal worth
- A guarantee of financial success

## Current Scorecard Inputs

The current website scorecard asks users to enter monthly and account-level numbers such as:

- Monthly income
- Monthly bills or needs
- Monthly wants or lifestyle spending
- Monthly savings
- Monthly debt payments or debt-related input
- Total debt
- Emergency fund or savings balance
- Investing or contribution amount

Exact input names should match the current website code before publishing the methodology publicly.

## Current Scoring Instruments

### 1. Cash Flow Control

Cash Flow Control measures whether the pilot has money remaining after monthly outflow.

Why it matters:

Positive cash flow gives the household breathing room. Negative cash flow means the pilot is likely drifting into debt, stress, or missed goals.

Simple public explanation:

> Cash flow shows whether your money has room to breathe after monthly expenses.

### 2. Savings System

Savings System measures whether income is being converted into future capacity.

Why it matters:

A savings habit creates options. Even a small repeatable contribution builds runway and confidence.

Simple public explanation:

> Savings show whether part of your income is being used to build future control.

### 3. Emergency Runway

Emergency Runway measures how much financial buffer exists for surprise expenses or income disruption.

Why it matters:

Emergency savings helps prevent one unexpected expense from becoming new debt.

Simple public explanation:

> Emergency runway shows how much breathing room you have before life turbulence hits.

### 4. Debt Load

Debt Load measures how much debt pressure is present relative to the pilot's monthly financial picture.

Important audit note:

The current website label should avoid calling this formal Debt-to-Income unless the formula uses monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income.

If the formula uses total debt balance compared to monthly income, label it as Debt Load instead.

Simple public explanation:

> Debt load shows how much pressure debt is putting on your flight plan.

### 5. Wealth Fuel

Wealth Fuel measures whether the pilot is putting money toward longer-term assets, investing, or future ownership.

Why it matters:

Once the basics are stable, consistent wealth fuel helps the pilot move from survival to ownership.

Simple public explanation:

> Wealth fuel shows whether future goals are receiving consistent attention.

## Score Categories

Recommended simplified public status categories:

| Score Range | Status | Meaning |
|---:|---|---|
| 0 to 49 | Pre-Flight | The pilot needs clarity, control, and a basic plan. |
| 50 to 74 | Stabilizing | The pilot has some control but still has key pressure points. |
| 75 to 89 | Cruise Control | The pilot has solid momentum and should focus on consistency. |
| 90 to 100 | Flight Mode | The pilot has strong control and can focus on growth, ownership, and optimization. |

Note:

If the current website uses Grounded, Takeoff Ready, Cruising, and Flight Mode, the SOP should decide whether to keep those labels or migrate toward the brand journey terms.

## Public Explanation Rule

The public result page should never explain every formula.

Use this structure:

### Why this score?

Your score reflects four core signals:

1. Cash flow
2. Emergency runway
3. Debt pressure
4. Savings and future fuel

Then show short signal labels:

- Cash Flow: Strong, Building, or Needs Attention
- Emergency Runway: Strong, Building, or Low
- Debt Load: Low, Moderate, or Elevated
- Savings System: On Course, Building, or Needs Attention

## Next 3 Moves Rule

Every result should produce three next moves.

Rules:

1. Give no more than three actions.
2. Use plain English.
3. Start with the highest-risk area.
4. Keep the tone direct but respectful.
5. Avoid shame language.
6. Point the pilot toward the Flight Plan Budget System.

Example:

1. Build your first emergency runway.
2. Protect positive monthly cash flow.
3. Pick one debt target before the next payday.

## Evidence Library

The scoring system should be validated using trusted financial education and household finance sources.

Recommended sources:

- Federal Reserve Economic Well-Being of U.S. Households
- CFPB emergency savings guidance
- CFPB debt-to-income education
- CFPB Financial Well-Being Scale
- BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey
- Fidelity long-term savings guideline
- Fannie Mae and housing finance DTI context

## Research Use Standard

Use research to support the methodology, not to overwhelm the pilot.

Classify every threshold as one of the following:

1. Research-supported benchmark
2. National reference data
3. FFM methodology decision
4. Behavioral design choice

## Validation SOP

### Step 1: Capture Current Formula

Before changing the website, document:

- All inputs
- All calculations
- All point weights
- All score caps
- Status category logic
- Recommendation logic
- Edge cases

### Step 2: Audit Labels

Confirm every public label accurately describes the formula.

Priority issue:

If the code does not calculate formal DTI, do not label the metric Debt-to-Income.

Use Debt Load unless formal DTI is implemented.

### Step 3: Validate Thresholds

Review each threshold against the evidence library.

Document whether the threshold is:

- Supported by external source
- Based on FFM educational judgment
- Needing revision

### Step 4: Stress Test Scenarios

Run the formula against realistic household profiles:

1. Negative cash flow
2. Paycheck-to-paycheck
3. Low debt but no savings
4. High income with high debt
5. Strong savings but weak cash flow
6. One-month emergency runway
7. Three-month emergency runway
8. Strong Flight Mode household

### Step 5: Review Pilot Experience

Confirm the public-facing explanation remains simple.

Pass standard:

The pilot can understand their score, status, and next move in under 60 seconds.

### Step 6: Version Control

Every scoring update should include:

- Version number
- Date
- Reason for change
- Formula change summary
- Files changed
- Reviewer approval

## Education-Only Disclaimer

The Financial Flight Score is for educational purposes only. It is not financial, investment, tax, legal, debt, or credit advice. Users should review major financial decisions with a qualified professional when needed.

## Internal Rule

Do not make the Scorecard more impressive at the cost of clarity.

The trust win is not showing people how complex the formula is.

The trust win is helping a pilot say:

> I understand where I stand, why I received this score, and what I should do next.
